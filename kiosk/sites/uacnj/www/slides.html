Earth has a natural radio mirror in the sky

The ionosphere is a layer of electrically charged gas stretching from about 60 to 1,000 km above Earth's surface. The Sun's ultraviolet and X-ray radiation ionizes the upper atmosphere, and those charged particles reflect certain radio waves back toward Earth — making long-distance radio communication possible.

  • Solar flares and geomagnetic storms shake up the ionosphere
  • Those disturbances can disrupt GPS signals, aviation radio, and power grids
  • The very same events that paint the sky with auroras also change how radio waves travel around the world

Bottom-right screen: A live map of radio signals

Every dot on that map is a real radio signal that reached the UACNJ antenna from somewhere around the world, just moments ago. The signals use a mode called WSPR (pronounced "whisper") — Weak Signal Propagation Reporter — designed specifically to travel as far as possible using just a tiny amount of power.

  • Amateur radio operators around the globe transmit these signals automatically, 24 hours a day
  • When the ionosphere is healthy and calm, signals arrive from far and wide
  • During a solar storm, the pattern shifts — some paths open up, others go dark

Bottom-left screen: The HF radio spectrum, live

This colorful "waterfall" display is showing every radio signal between 0 and 30 MHz — the entire shortwave and amateur radio band — all at once, streaming in real time.

  • Left to right: radio frequency, like sweeping across a very wide radio dial
  • Top to bottom: time passing — the newest signals are always at the top
  • Bright colors mean strong signals; dark areas are quiet
  • Try spotting individual radio stations, amateur contacts, and the faint background hiss of the ionosphere itself

Amateur radio operators as citizen scientists

By collecting observations from hundreds of receiving stations around the world at the same moment, scientists can watch a solar disturbance ripple through the ionosphere in real time — a little like the way a network of seismometers tracks an earthquake spreading across the globe.

  • The receiver here at UACNJ is one node in that worldwide network
  • Amateur radio operators voluntarily contribute millions of observations every single day
  • This data helps researchers forecast space weather, protect satellites, and keep astronauts safe

Where radio meets the night sky

HamSCI — the Ham Radio Science Citizen Investigation — is a collaboration between amateur radio operators and professional researchers at universities and observatories across North America. Together, they use the global amateur radio network as a scientific instrument to study the ionosphere and space weather.

  • This Personal Space Weather Station (PSWS) was built and is maintained by the HamSCI team at the University of Scranton
  • UACNJ's quiet, dark-sky hilltop is ideal for both optical and radio astronomy — far from city noise and interference
  • Optical and radio astronomers have always been neighbors: both are simply studying the universe at different wavelengths

Want to learn more? Visit hamsci.org
